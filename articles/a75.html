Discover the Secrets of the A75: France’s Spectacular Route of Volcanoes

The A75 motorway is more than a fast route between Clermont-Ferrand and Béziers—it is one of France’s most dramatic road trips, crossing volcanic landscapes, high plateaus, deep valleys, and engineering marvels. Known by many travelers as the “Route des Volcans,” it offers a rare mix of natural wonder, cultural heritage, and unforgettable viewpoints.

Why the A75 Is One of France’s Most Scenic Motorways

The A75, also called La Méridienne, runs through the heart of the Massif Central, one of the oldest and most fascinating geological regions in Europe. Unlike many motorways built mainly for speed, this route feels like a journey through open-air history, where ancient volcanic activity has shaped the land for millions of years.

Starting near Clermont-Ferrand, the road quickly reveals the silhouettes of the Chaîne des Puys, a UNESCO World Heritage volcanic chain. These rounded cones and craters are extinct volcanoes, but their presence still dominates the horizon.

What makes the A75 especially attractive is that much of it is toll-free, except for the famous Millau Viaduct section. This makes it a practical and budget-friendly option for travelers heading south while still enjoying exceptional scenery.

Highlights along the route include:

  • The volcanic peaks near Clermont-Ferrand
  • The wide-open landscapes of the Cézallier and Aubrac plateaus
  • The impressive descent toward the Tarn Valley
  • The architectural wonder of the Millau Viaduct
  • Easy access to villages, regional parks, and hiking trails

The Volcanic Landscapes You Should Not Miss

The “Route des Volcans” nickname is well deserved, particularly in the northern part of the A75. The surrounding landscapes were formed by eruptions, lava flows, and erosion over thousands of years, creating a distinctive terrain unlike anywhere else in France.

A key stop is the Chaîne des Puys, located west of Clermont-Ferrand. The most famous volcano here is the Puy de Dôme, which rises to 1,465 meters and offers panoramic views over dozens of volcanic cones. Visitors can reach the summit via the Panoramique des Dômes train, making it accessible even for those who do not want a long hike.

Further south, the scenery changes but remains equally striking. The Cézallier plateau feels almost untouched, with rolling pastures, stone farmhouses, and wide skies. Then comes the Aubrac, a high volcanic plateau known for its wild beauty, traditional cattle farming, and peaceful walking routes.

Recommended stops for nature lovers:

  • Puy de Dôme for iconic volcanic views
  • Vulcania, an educational theme park dedicated to volcanoes
  • Lac Pavin, a deep volcanic lake with a mysterious atmosphere
  • Saint-Flour, a historic town built on a volcanic basalt outcrop
  • Aubrac Regional Natural Park, perfect for hiking and photography

Culture, Food, and Hidden Villages Along the A75

One of the best-kept secrets of the A75 is how easily it connects travelers to authentic rural France. Just a short detour from the motorway can lead to medieval towns, Romanesque churches, local markets, and traditional inns.

In Auvergne, food is deeply tied to the land. Cheeses such as Saint-Nectaire, Cantal, Bleu d’Auvergne, and Salers are regional treasures. Many villages along the route serve hearty mountain dishes designed for travelers, hikers, and farmers.
