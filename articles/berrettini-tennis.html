Matteo Berrettini: Is the "Roman Hammer" Ready to Reclaim the Tennis Throne?

After two years of battling recurring injuries and slipping down the ATP rankings, the charismatic Italian powerhouse is finally showing signs of a major comeback. As the tennis world looks for a new hierarchy, many are wondering if the former Wimbledon finalist still possesses the physical durability to match his elite shot-making.

The Resilience of a Champion: Overcoming the Injury Plague

The career of Matteo Berrettini has been a rollercoaster of soaring highs and frustrating physical setbacks. Since reaching the Wimbledon final in 2021, the Roman has faced surgery on his right hand, abdominal strains, and severe ankle issues that forced him out of several Grand Slams. For a player whose game relies on explosive power, these interruptions did more than just affect his ranking; they tested his mental fortitude.

However, 2024 has marked a turning point. Instead of rushing back, Berrettini has adopted a more surgical approach to his calendar. His recent success on the Challenger circuit and ATP 250 events proves that while his body may have been fragile, his competitive instinct remains razor-sharp. He isn't just playing again; he is winning with the conviction of a top-10 player.

The Weapons: Why the "Hammer" Still Scares the Tour

What makes Berrettini such a threat to the current dominance of players like Jannik Sinner or Carlos Alcaraz? It comes down to his "big game" DNA. In an era of elite baseliners, Matteo brings an old-school brutality that is difficult to neutralize.
  • The Serve: Clocking regularly over 220 km/h, his first serve remains one of the most unreturnable shots in tennis.
  • The Forehand: Specifically nicknamed the "Hammer," his ability to dictate points from the baseline puts immediate pressure on opponents.
  • The Slice: Often underrated, his backhand slice is a tactical masterpiece, keeping the ball low and forcing power-hitters out of their comfort zone.
  • Grass Court Mastery: His game is naturally suited for fast surfaces, making him a perennial dark horse at any tournament played on turf.

The Mental Shift: Maturity Over Momentum

One of the most significant changes in Berrettini’s recent performances is his tactical maturity. In his early years, he relied almost exclusively on brute force. Today, we see a player who manages his energy levels more effectively throughout a match. He has learned to navigate the "empty" moments of a set, focusing on high-percentage tennis when his physical reserves are low.

Off the court, the Roman has also sought a better balance. By restructuring his coaching staff and focusing heavily on specialized physiotherapy, he is treating his body like a high-performance engine that requires constant maintenance. This shift from "surviving the season" to "peaking for milestones" is the hallmark of a veteran ready for one final, great run.

Can He Compete with the "New Gen" of Sinner and Alcaraz?

The landscape of the ATP Tour has shifted during Berrettini's absence. His compatriot Jannik Sinner has risen to World No. 1, and Carlos Alcaraz has redefined speed on the court. To return to the top, Matteo doesn't necessarily need to be faster than them—he needs to be more dominant in his own service games.

When healthy, Berrettini possesses a "ceiling" that few can reach. His performance in recent tournaments shows that he can still overpower top-20 players. The challenge will be his consistency over five-set matches in the heat of a Grand Slam second week. If his ankles and core hold up, he remains the one veteran the young stars desperately want to avoid in a draw.

Conclusion: A Roar Heard Round the World

Matteo Berrettini is no longer the "next big thing"; he is a seasoned warrior with a point to prove. The "Roman Hammer" has shown that his spirit is as heavy as his forehand, and the tennis world is objectively better when he is fit and firing. Whether he can return to a Grand Slam final remains to be seen, but one thing is certain: he is no longer just a name on the entry list
